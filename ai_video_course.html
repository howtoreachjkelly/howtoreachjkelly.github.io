The Product Map

Before diving into specific tools, understand how the space is organized. There are distinct categories that serve different parts of production. Conflating them is the main reason people buy the wrong thing.

How The Stack Is Organized

AI video production tools fall into five functional categories. Each solves a different problem, and the best pipelines combine tools from multiple categories rather than trying to find one that does everything.

Category 1: Generative Video Models

These are the core of the space. They take text prompts, reference images, or existing video and generate new footage. The main distinction is text-to-video (generate from a prompt alone) vs. image-to-video (animate a still) vs. video-to-video (restyle or extend existing footage). Most professional use is image-to-video because it gives more control over the result.

Category 2: Voice Synthesis & Cloning

Tools that generate narration, dialogue, or fully dubbed audio tracks. Ranges from simple text-to-speech (Murf) to professional voice cloning with emotional range (ElevenLabs) to full avatar-synchronized lip-sync (HeyGen, Synthesia).

Category 3: Avatar & Talking Head Tools

Generate photorealistic or stylized human presenters from a script. Primarily used for corporate training, marketing videos, and multilingual content at scale. Synthesia and HeyGen dominate here.

Category 4: NLE + AI Editing

Traditional non-linear editing tools (Premiere Pro, DaVinci Resolve, Final Cut Pro) that have added significant AI features — auto-captioning, generative fill, object removal, reframing. These are post-production tools enhanced by AI, not AI-native tools.

Category 5: Motion Graphics & Compositing

After Effects (dominant), Cavalry (data-driven), Blender (3D/open source). These are for titles, animated graphics, VFX, and branded motion. AI has accelerated these workflows but hasn't replaced the underlying skill requirement.

Category 6: Orchestration Platforms

Tools like ComfyUI, Martini, and Krea Nodes that let you chain multiple models together in a workflow. Think of them as DAWs for AI generation — you route prompts through multiple models, compare outputs, and connect generation to editing. More relevant as your sophistication grows.

Market Note — June 2026

Sora is being discontinued. OpenAI shut down the Sora web app and announced API deprecation later in 2026. If you encounter it in conversations or RFPs, it's relevant to know it existed and why — but it's not a current planning tool. Its successors for photorealism are Veo 3.1 and Runway Gen-4.

The Three Generation Modes — Know These Cold

Mode What It Does Typical Use Control Level
Text-to-Video (T2V) Generates footage from a written prompt alone Concept exploration, B-roll, abstract content Low — output is less predictable
Image-to-Video (I2V) Animates a provided still image Most professional production work High — you control the visual starting point

The Current Frontier (June 2026)

Native audio in a single generation pass — video + sound effects + ambient noise — is now table stakes for the leading models. Veo 3.1, Kling 3.0, Seedance 2.0, and Wan 2.7 all do this. A year ago it required a separate step. Factor this into any workflow design conversation.

Key Limitations That Haven't Been Solved

Being fluent in what these tools can't do is just as important as knowing what they can. Current persistent weaknesses across all models:

  • Physics consistency: Water, fabric, smoke behave plausibly but not accurately across longer clips
  • Hand rendering: Fingers remain difficult in close-ups — improve prompt specificity or frame wide
  • Character consistency across scenes: The same character may drift visually between shots. Mitigation: image-to-video with a locked reference, or models like Seedance with Universal Reference
  • Clip length: Most models cap at 5-15 seconds. Veo 3.1 and Kling push longer but chaining clips is still the primary approach for longer-form content
  • Narrative coherence: Generating a story arc across multiple shots still requires significant human sequencing and editorial work

Day 2, Part 1

Script & Pre-Production AI

The most underrated part of the AI video stack. What happens before you touch a generation tool directly determines output quality. AI tools for scripting and pre-production have a higher ROI per hour than most generation tools.

Script AI: What's Actually Useful

General-purpose LLMs (Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini) are the dominant scriptwriting tools — not specialized video-script apps. Specialized tools add structure and prompting guidance but aren't necessary once you know how to prompt. What matters is how you use them.

How To Use LLMs For Video Scripts (The Working Method)

Step 1
Brief the AI on format, audience, and constraint
Before asking for a script, give it: the target duration, the platform (social vs. long-form vs. training), the audience sophistication level, and one thing it must NOT do (e.g., "don't use the phrase 'dive deep'"). This shapes the register.
Step 2
Generate a shot list alongside the script
Ask for a two-column output: narration text on the left, visual description for each line on the right. The visual descriptions become your generation prompts. This also forces the AI to think about visual pacing.
Step 3
Convert visual descriptions into generation prompts
Each visual description from Step 2 gets converted into a structured generation prompt. Structure: [Subject + action] + [Environment + lighting] + [Camera movement] + [Mood/style]. The AI can do this for you — just ask.
Step 4
Storyboard with AI image generation
Before generating video (expensive, slow), generate static storyboard frames using Midjourney, Firefly, or DALL-E. Validate composition and style before spending generation credits. A 12-frame storyboard takes 10 minutes and saves hours.

Prompt Engineering for Video Generation

This is a distinct skill from general AI prompting. Video generation prompts have a specific structure that consistently outperforms freeform descriptions:

[SUBJECT]: A middle-aged woman in a tailored navy jacket [ACTION]: looks up from a laptop and turns directly toward camera, slight smile [ENVIRONMENT]: glass-walled office, city skyline visible through windows, late afternoon golden light [CAMERA]: slow push-in from medium to close-up [STYLE]: cinematic, 24fps, shallow depth of field, desaturated shadows [EXCLUDE]: text overlays, other people, motion blur

The Most Effective Single Technique

Use image-to-video rather than text-to-video whenever possible. Generate your exact reference image first (Midjourney, Firefly, DALL-E), then animate it. This gives you 80% control over the visual output before the video model even runs. T2V is exploration; I2V is production.

Workflow Practice

More hands-on this time. Two exercises require free accounts. One is purely analytical. Total: ~90 minutes.

EX 2.1 Script-to-Shot-List Pipeline 30 min

Build a 60-second video script with a matched shot list using Claude or ChatGPT.

  1. Use this brief: A 60-second marketing video for a premium LA-based architecture firm. Target audience: high-net-worth homeowners. Platform: website hero and Instagram. Tone: aspirational but grounded, not pretentious.
  2. Prompt your chosen LLM: "Write a 60-second marketing script for [brief]. Format it as a two-column table: left column is narration text, right column is a visual description for each line. After the script, convert each visual description into a structured video generation prompt using this format: [Subject + action] / [Environment + lighting] / [Camera movement] / [Mood/style]."
  3. Review the output. Edit 3 of the generation prompts to be more specific or more accurate to the brief. Identify which prompts would need image-to-video (I2V) vs. text-to-video (T2V) and why.
  4. Pick the strongest prompt and run it in Runway or Veo 3.1.
EX 2.2 The Image-to-Video Upgrade Test 30 min

Demonstrate the quality difference between T2V and I2V using the same concept.

  1. Choose any subject — a product, a location, a person.
  2. Run T2V: In Runway (or Pika), describe the subject in a text prompt and generate a clip.
  3. Generate a reference image: Go to Adobe Firefly (free tier), Ideogram, or DALL-E and generate the same subject as a still image. Download it.
  4. Run I2V: Upload your reference image to Runway and generate again using the same descriptive text as a motion prompt.
  5. Compare: Write down specifically what changed — visual consistency, quality, predictability. This is the foundational argument for I2V as a default workflow choice.
EX 2.3 Tool Selection Scenario (No Tools Needed) 30 min

Four production briefs. For each, write: which tools you'd use at each stage, why, and one thing you'd need to watch for.

  1. Brief A: A Fortune 500 HR team needs to produce 40 onboarding training videos in 12 languages within 90 days. No film crew. $50K budget.
  2. Brief B: An indie filmmaker needs 10 seconds of a photorealistic storm at sea for a feature film. Practical photography is not possible.
  3. Brief C: A DTC beauty brand needs 30 social video ads per month for Instagram and TikTok. Varied products, consistent brand look. $8K/month.
  4. Brief D: A tech startup needs a 90-second explainer video with a branded animated presenter, English and Spanish, for a product launch in 3 weeks. One designer on staff who knows Figma but not video.

There are no single correct answers. The goal is to practice the selection reasoning, not arrive at a perfect tool stack.

Day 3, Part 1

Tool Selection Logic

The difference between knowing tools and selecting tools. A decision framework for matching capabilities to requirements.

The Five Questions

Any tool selection conversation in AI video should work through these five questions in order. They sequence from constraint (eliminates options) to preference (distinguishes among remaining options).

Q1
What is the output format and destination?
Social (9:16, short, high-churn) vs. broadcast/streaming (16:9, long, high-fidelity) vs. enterprise training (avatar-friendly, LMS-exportable) vs. film/VFX (composited into live footage). This immediately segments the tool landscape.
Q2
Is there a real-person presenter requirement?
If yes: avatar tools (HeyGen, Synthesia) or real production. If no: generative video models. This question eliminates an entire category quickly.
Q3
What is the volume and cadence?
One-off / low volume: best-quality tool, pay per clip. High-volume / recurring: API-accessible tools with pricing models that scale (Runway API, Kling API, open-source local deployment). At sufficient volume, open-source local deployment (Wan via ComfyUI) often beats per-clip pricing.
Q4
Are there licensing or data compliance constraints?
Commercial brand work: Adobe Firefly (commercially safe training data). Enterprise/GDPR: Synthesia (European, strict compliance). Privacy-sensitive data: local deployment only. Chinese-origin tools (Kling, Seedance, CapCut/ByteDance): some enterprise policies restrict these.
Q5
What existing tools and skills does the team have?
Adobe ecosystem teams: Firefly integration in Premiere reduces friction. DaVinci teams: outputs from all major generators work seamlessly. Technical teams with GPU infrastructure: open-source local deployment is viable. Non-technical teams: cloud-based platforms with good UX (Pika, HeyGen) over ComfyUI.

Day 3 Exercises

Applied Practice

The capstone exercises. These produce real outputs you can show. Total: ~2 hours.

EX 3.1 Build a Complete 30-Second Spot 60 min

A complete production run from brief to assembled output. Use free tiers only — no cost required.

  1. Choose your subject: Pick something you know — a business, a product, a place. Real subjects are easier than invented ones because you know what's true about them.
  2. Script it: Use an LLM to write a 30-second (≈75 words) script with a two-column shot list. Edit until you'd actually say the words aloud.
  3. Generate 3 clips: Use Veo 3.1 (Google Vids), Runway, or Pika. Pick the 3 shots from your shot list that would benefit most from AI generation. Run them. You don't need all 8-10 shots — just 3.
  4. Record voiceover: Either use ElevenLabs (free tier) to generate it or record it yourself. Your voice on a phone mic is fine.
  5. Assemble: Use CapCut (free) or DaVinci Resolve (free). Put clips in order, lay VO track under it, add titles if you want. Export at 1080p.
  6. Critique your output: Watch it back. What broke? What worked? What would a second pass fix first?
EX 3.2 Prompt Iteration Log 30 min

The fastest way to learn prompt engineering: systematic variation tracking.

  1. Choose a single subject and platform (e.g., Runway Gen-4).
  2. Generate version 1 with a basic prompt. Save it.
  3. Change one variable: add a specific camera movement. Generate version 2.
  4. Change one variable: change the lighting descriptor. Generate version 3.
  5. Change one variable: add a negative prompt. Generate version 4.
  6. Compare all four. Write one sentence on what each change actually did to the output. This exercise teaches you to isolate variables — the core skill in prompt engineering.
EX 3.3 The Stakeholder Brief 30 min

Write a one-page brief recommending an AI video tool stack to a fictional stakeholder. This tests whether you can synthesize and communicate the landscape.

  1. Pick one scenario:
    A: A VP at a media company asking whether to invest in AI video tools for their content production team (30 people, mixed editorial and marketing output)
    B: A startup founder building a content marketing engine with 2 people and $3K/month
    C: A university considering AI tools for online course video production across 40 departments
  2. Write a one-page (300-400 word) recommendation covering: recommended tool stack, rationale for each choice, one tool you explicitly excluded and why, and one risk they should plan for.
  3. Do not hedge every statement. Make a call and justify it.

What To Say When Rankings Come Up

Benchmark rankings (Elo scores, leaderboards) update weekly and flip regularly. The right framing is: "The rankings move fast enough that I focus on which model category wins a use case rather than which scored highest last month." This is more accurate and more credible than citing a specific number.
